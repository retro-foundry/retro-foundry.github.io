Stunt Car Racer — Physics Bounciness Deep Review

Reference: reference/StuntCarRacer.s (Amiga 68000)  ·  Port: src/Car_Behaviour.cpp  ·  Date: 18 April 2026

Short answer: The steady-state math is correct and the integration architecture is sound. The bounce feel is almost certainly softer than the Amiga original — not because of a bug, but because a well-sampled 60 Hz discrete spring-damper is inherently less oscillatory than an undersampled ~7 Hz one. Tuning PhysicsConfig.h is the right lever; see §6.

1 The Amiga Spring-Damper (heart of the bounce)

The entire per-wheel suspension lives in one six-instruction subroutine — calculate.difference at StuntCarRacer.s:15354: 

calculate.difference
    move.w  #276,d3          ; spring constant
    beq     .zero
    muls    d3,d0            ; d0 = (new_penetration − old_penetration) × 276
    asr.l   #8,d0            ; ÷ 256
.zero
    add.w   d6,d0            ; + raw penetration depth
    rts

d0 enters as Δd = new_penetration − old_penetration (velocity term); d6 holds the current raw penetration depth (position term). The resulting force formula is:

force = (Δd × 276) ÷ 256 + d

where Δd = penetration change since last tick, d = current penetration depth

The effective damping coefficient is 256/256 = 1.0 (the position term is passed through unchanged). This runs once per game tick at ≈ 7.14 Hz — the Amiga waits for MIN.FRAMES = 5 PAL vblanks, but the render loop typically costs ~7 frames, giving the reference period PHYSICS_REFERENCE_STEP_SECONDS = 0.14 s.

After the three wheel forces are computed, they are averaged (average.amount.below.road) and projected onto the car's surface normal via calculate.car.collision.acceleration to produce world-axis accelerations. Everything else flows from there.

2 The Port's Spring-Damper at 60 Hz

CalculateWheelCollision() in Car_Behaviour.cpp reproduces the Amiga formula faithfully, with one critical adaptation for the higher tick rate: 

const long spring_effective =
    (g_physicsStepScale > 0.0f)
        ? (long)((float)spring / g_physicsStepScale)
        : spring;

At 60 Hz, g_physicsStepScale = stepSeconds / 0.14 ≈ 0.01667 / 0.14 ≈ 0.119. The velocity term Δd is ~8.4× smaller per substep (the penetration changes less in 16.7 ms than in 140 ms), so the spring coefficient must be scaled up by the same factor to preserve total impulse:

spring_effective = 276 ÷ 0.119 ≈ 2319

Force-per-step is smaller, but there are 8.4× more steps — net impulse per reference period is preserved.

The position (damping) term uses raw damping = 256, which is correct — penetration depth does not scale with timestep. The 16-bit signed-word arithmetic (short delta_word, short spring_word, etc.) faithfully mirrors the Amiga's muls / asr.l / add.w overflow behaviour.

3 Why It Is Still Less Bouncy

Even with per-step forces mathematically preserved, the transient dynamics differ because the suspension is a discrete feedback loop:

penetration => force => acceleration => speed => position => new penetration => ···

At 7 Hz, the loop takes a few, large steps through each oscillation cycle. Before the next tick corrects it, the car overshoots the equilibrium significantly. That overshoot is the Amiga bounce. With spring = 276 and damping = 256 at 7 Hz the system is underdamped — it oscillates visibly before settling.

At 60 Hz, the same loop takes many small steps. The system converges toward the continuous-time solution — smoother and more critically damped. Overshoots are caught and corrected within a fraction of the oscillation period. The car settles faster. This is not a scaling error; it is the fundamental behaviour of discrete feedback systems under refinement.

The Amiga's bounce was partly intentional tuning and partly an artefact of the low tick rate. The port at 60 Hz eliminates the artefact while preserving the intention.

More formally, the damping ratio ζ of a discrete spring-damper increases as the timestep shrinks for the same coefficients:

ζ ∝ c / (2·√(k·m)) → higher ζ at shorter Δt for fixed k, c

k = spring constant, c = damping coefficient, m = effective mass

61c5712 Fractional Carry Physics — Car_Behaviour.cpp

Good change. Replaced naive (long)((float)value × stepScale) truncation with a 16.16 fixed-point accumulator. At 60 Hz, stepScale ≈ 0.119, so most small integer values would previously truncate to zero every single substep, silently leaking energy out of the simulation. The carry accumulator ensures nothing is lost across substeps: 
// DistributeStepValueWithScale — 16.16 fixed-point carry
const long scale_fp = (long)(step_scale * 65536.0f + 0.5f);
long long total = remainder + (full_step_value * (long long)scale_fp);
substep_value   = (long)(total / 65536LL);
remainder       = (long)(total - substep_value * 65536LL);

All twelve integration paths (rotation speed, world speed, position × XYZ, angle × XYZ) were updated. Carry state is included in the instance state saved/restored for multiplayer.

Before this, the AI car would have suffered the same integer-truncation energy loss as the player. Opponent physics now run on the same precision footing.
